Fame Selects for the Wrong People

June 15, 2026

writing careers

The people best equipped to survive fame are often the least likely to achieve it.

Fame is usually presented as a reward for doing something remarkable. Build a successful product, make a piece of art people care about, say something at the right moment, and attention arrives as proof that the work mattered. From the outside, that attention looks overwhelmingly useful. It brings money, reach, influence, access, and opportunities that would otherwise take years to accumulate.

What gets overlooked is that fame does not simply increase the number of people who appreciate the work. It increases the number of people who feel entitled to evaluate the person behind it.

Once enough attention arrives, the work is no longer the only thing being judged. Your personality becomes part of the product. Your motives become public property. Old comments are pulled out of context, minor decisions become evidence of character, and strangers begin constructing a complete version of you from fragments. Some will admire that version. Others will despise it. Neither group needs to know much about you for the emotional force of their judgement to feel real.

The people most capable of absorbing this are usually those who are not particularly dependent on public approval. They have a stable identity outside the audience. They do not search their own name every night. They do not experience every criticism as a referendum on their worth. They have friends, routines, and obligations that would continue to exist if the attention disappeared tomorrow.

They are also less likely to spend their life broadcasting themselves.

That is the contradiction. The personality most protected from fame is often the personality least motivated to pursue visibility. If you are reasonably self-contained, more interested in the work than the reaction to it, and generally uninterested in performing your life online, then the machinery required to become famous feels unnatural. Publishing constantly, repeating yourself, networking, packaging the work, and maintaining a public presence can feel like noise around the thing you actually care about.

Unfortunately, exceptional work is not self-distributing. The world does not possess some hidden mechanism that notices quality and quietly routes attention toward it. Work becomes visible because somebody makes it visible. If the person who created it refuses to promote it, then discovery depends on luck, an unusually enthusiastic third party, or some external event large enough to drag it into public view.

The opposite personality has a much easier time becoming visible. People who are highly responsive to attention are more likely to pursue it. Likes, comments, follower counts, praise, and recognition provide immediate feedback that broadcasting is working. That feedback encourages more publishing, more performance, more experimentation, and eventually more reach.

The same sensitivity that drives the person toward fame can make fame difficult to survive.

Public attention does not remain positive. The distribution system that carries praise also carries mockery, accusations, entitlement, obsessive scrutiny, and the general behaviour of strangers who have mistaken access for intimacy. Once an audience becomes large enough, a percentage of it will dislike you regardless of what you do. Some criticism will be deserved. Some will be based on misunderstanding. Some will exist because millions of people contain a statistically reliable number of lunatics with internet connections.

A person whose motivation is tightly connected to public response now has a problem. The mechanism that brought them success has become the mechanism delivering hostility directly into their nervous system.

Dong Nguyen, the creator of Flappy Bird, is one of the clearest examples. He made a small game that suddenly became a global phenomenon. It generated substantial revenue and attracted enormous attention, but the attention did not stop at the game. People accused him of theft, addiction, manipulation, and dishonesty. He received abuse and threats. Eventually he removed the game from sale and withdrew from the spectacle around it.

He built a game. The internet turned him into an event.

The same pattern appears elsewhere when small teams create something that unexpectedly escapes the scale they were prepared for. The developers of SUPERHOT VR found themselves dealing not only with commercial success, but with the expectations, criticism, and political interpretation that arrive when a project becomes culturally visible. A small group can suddenly find itself responding to an audience orders of magnitude larger than the organisation that produced the work. The product may scale instantly. The people do not.

Commercial success does not prepare someone for that transition. A creator may know how to maintain servers, fix bugs, fulfil orders, manage revenue, or hire staff. None of that teaches a private person how to process thousands of strangers discussing their personality, competence, appearance, motives, and moral worth.

Ordinary social instincts are badly calibrated for this environment. Human judgement evolved in groups small enough that reputation had context. If ten people in your village hated you, that probably mattered. If ten people among a million viewers hate you, it may mean nothing more than the audience has become large enough for basic probability to take over.

The nervous system does not reliably understand the distinction.

This creates a selection problem. The qualities that make someone discoverable are not the same qualities that make them durable after discovery. To become visible deliberately, a person needs enough interest in attention to pursue distribution. To survive visibility, they need enough distance from attention that the reaction does not consume them.

The ideal public figure would want reach without needing approval. They would promote the work relentlessly while remaining emotionally detached from the image being promoted. They would listen to useful criticism without granting every stranger authority. They would remain visible enough to grow, but absent enough to preserve some private sense of self.

That is an unusually narrow psychological position.

It also explains why deliberate reputation-building feels uncomfortable to many people who are otherwise well suited to influence. A private person may have the judgement, stability, and emotional distance required to survive scrutiny, but they resent the performance required to become visible. They assume that good work should be enough. It is a pleasant belief, and a very effective way to remain unknown.

The answer is not to become indifferent to everyone, nor is it to retreat completely. It is to separate visibility from identity.

Publish the work, but do not live inside the reaction to it. Build a public surface without confusing it for the whole person. Let attention be information rather than emotional nutrition. Keep relationships, routines, and responsibilities that existed before the audience and would remain if the audience disappeared.

Most importantly, understand that fame is not simply a larger version of being appreciated. It is a different operating environment with different failure modes. The audience is not your community. Reach is not intimacy. Visibility is not understanding.

The people best suited to fame may be those who could live comfortably without it. But unless they deliberately create some mechanism for the work to travel, nobody will ever know they were suited to it at all.
