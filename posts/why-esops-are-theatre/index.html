Why ESOPs Are Theatre

November 21, 2025

startups governance incentives

Equity is one of the most powerful stories in the startup world. Founders and investors use it to attract talent, justify long hours, and create the impression that everyone is “in it together.” The problem is that the story often doesn’t match the reality.

Many ESOPs are not mechanisms for meaningful ownership. They are theatre.

The promise vs the structure

The pitch is familiar: join early, work hard, and you’ll own a meaningful slice of the company. If it succeeds, you succeed. It sounds empowering. It feels fair.

But the actual structure of many ESOPs tells a different story:

  • High strike prices
  • Complex vesting schedules
  • Unclear paths to liquidity
  • Board discretion on exercising options
  • Dilution from future raises
  • No voting rights
  • No real influence over governance

On paper, the employee “owns” part of the company. In practice, they often hold a theoretical claim that may never convert into anything tangible.

Options without liquidity are promises without timelines

The biggest flaw in most ESOPs is the absence of a predictable liquidity event. Employees may vest options for years, only to find that:

  • The company isn’t planning an exit
  • Secondary sales are restricted
  • Buyback mechanisms don’t exist
  • The strike price has risen to the point where exercising is unrealistic

If the only path to value is a hypothetical future acquisition or IPO, the ESOP becomes functionally symbolic.

Power sits upstream

Equity suggests participation in ownership, but ownership without influence is not true ownership.

Employees with ESOP allocations rarely have:

  • Decision rights
  • Visibility into the cap table
  • Insight into fundraising terms
  • Influence over board composition
  • Access to governance information

The people doing the work hold “ownership” only in a technical sense. The people making the decisions hold the real power. That asymmetry makes the ESOP more of a motivational tool than a genuine transfer of ownership.

ESOPs justify behaviour they don’t compensate

One of the reasons ESOPs persist in their current form is because they serve a psychological function. They allow leaders to ask for sacrifice:

  • Longer hours
  • Higher pressure
  • Below-market salaries
  • Shifting priorities
  • Sustained commitment through uncertainty

Employees tolerate this because they believe they are working toward something bigger. Whether the structure supports that belief is often left unexamined.

Alignment breaks when the story breaks

Companies rely on ESOPs to align incentives, but alignment only works when:

  • Employees understand what their equity is worth
  • They believe they can access that value
  • They trust leadership to honour the spirit of the arrangement

When these conditions are missing, ESOPs stop being a tool for alignment and start being a source of quiet resentment.

A demotivated employee with “ownership” on paper is more damaging than a motivated employee with none.

What a meaningful ESOP looks like

Not all equity programs are theatre. Some companies treat them seriously. The difference is structural, not rhetorical.

A meaningful ESOP includes:

  • Transparent communication about valuation
  • Reasonable strike prices
  • Paths to liquidity that employees can realistically access
  • Clear buyback policies
  • Education about taxation and exercise risk
  • Honest acknowledgment of dilution and future funding rounds
  • A culture where employees are treated as co-owners, not just symbolic ones

When these pieces are in place, an ESOP becomes a real incentive. Without them, it’s just another benefit that sounds impressive but delivers little.

The point isn’t to abandon ESOPs

Equity can be an effective tool. It can create alignment. It can reward long-term commitment. But using ESOPs responsibly requires clarity about what they are and honesty about what they are not.

Theatre becomes harmful when companies start believing their own script.

An ESOP only matters if employees have a genuine path to value. Otherwise, it’s a narrative device used to extract more effort for less cost.

For an organisation built on trust and shared outcomes, symbolism isn’t enough. Ownership has to mean something real.
