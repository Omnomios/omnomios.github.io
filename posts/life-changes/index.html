Surviving the Big Changes

May 31, 2026

relationships ethics

There are moments in life that split the timeline. Losing a job. Getting divorced. A death in the family. The end of a relationship that had become part of your daily shape. The kind of event that does not merely inconvenience you, but rearranges the structure of your life so completely that you can point back to it years later and say: that was where it changed.

I have been through a few of those now. Five, by my count. Enough to notice the pattern, and enough to know that the first time is usually the worst because you are not just dealing with the event itself. You are dealing with the shock of discovering that your life can change that quickly.

The later ones are not easy. That would be the wrong word. They are still brutal. But they become more familiar. You start to recognise the shape of the storm. You know the first few weeks will be the hardest. You know your mind will try to solve the whole thing at once. You know the silence will become dangerous if you let it get too large. You know the heaviness will eventually lift, even if it refuses to feel temporary while you are inside it.

This is the strategy I use now.

The first thing is to simplify. The early stage of a major life change is not the time to prove how much load you can carry. Your brain is already in overdrive trying to resolve the event, model the future, re-run the past, assign blame, calculate risk, and keep you functional enough to answer messages like a normal human being. Adding more complexity to that state is how you drown.

So remove what you can remove. Take time off if you can. Pause obligations that do not matter. Stop talking to the friend who only calls to unload their own chaos. Avoid making large irreversible decisions while your nervous system is trying to put the furniture back after the earthquake. The point is not to retreat from life permanently. The point is to reduce the number of active fires while you work out which parts of your life are still standing.

Simplification is not weakness. It is triage.

Once the immediate load is reduced, find something to focus on. A major life change creates a vacuum. Something that used to provide structure disappears, and if you do not replace that structure deliberately, your mind will fill the space with self-attack. It will start reminding you of every mistake, every failure, every way you could have avoided this if you had only been smarter, stronger, kinder, richer, thinner, faster, or whatever other nonsense the little demon in your skull is selling that day.

Do not give it an empty room to perform in. Give yourself a mission.

If you are out of a job, get up each day and work on the same project. Set a goal with a deliverable. Build something. Write something. Clean up the portfolio. Train. Study. Apply. Do the work in a way that creates forward motion you can see. Do not just rot on the sofa and let the day become a fog of half-watched videos and increasingly dramatic thoughts about your own uselessness.

If the loss is personal, the mission may need to sit outside yourself. Give to something. Care for something. Create a small duty that requires you to keep showing up.

There was a period in my life where I was in a deep depression after a major change. I had a flock of chickens at the time. Ridiculous little dinosaurs, really. Every morning they needed food, water, and some basic level of care. They did not care how I felt. They did not need me to have a five-year plan. They needed me to get out of bed and open the coop.

That helped more than I expected. Caring for them gave the day a starting point. It put my attention somewhere other than myself. It was not a cure in the grand cinematic sense, because life is rude enough to resist clean story structure, but it was an anchor. Some mornings that is enough.

The next part is people. We are social animals, despite our best efforts to pretend otherwise. There is something stabilising about being around other humans when your internal world has become too loud. Not necessarily deep conversations. Not necessarily emotional excavation with candles and acoustic guitar, spare us all. Sometimes it is dinner. Sometimes it is sitting on the sofa playing video games. Sometimes it is being in the same room as someone safe while neither of you says much of anything.

The demons do better in silence and shadow. Fill both where you can.

This does not mean surrounding yourself with noise or pretending everything is fine. It means not letting isolation become the default environment. Pain bends perception when it has no outside reference point. Other people help correct the signal. They remind you, often without trying, that the world is still there and you are still in it.

The final part is remembering. When you are inside one of these changes, it feels permanent. The heaviness becomes so complete that you start to believe it has always been there and always will be. That is one of grief’s little tricks, because apparently suffering needed user-hostile design.

You have to remember that there was a time before this. Not as an exercise in nostalgia, and not to torture yourself with what is gone. Remember because it proves something important: your current state is not the whole of you. You have felt different before. You will feel different again.

A few months from now, the same event may still hurt, but it will not occupy the same amount of space. The mornings will become less heavy. The future will stop looking like one solid wall. You will catch yourself laughing at something stupid and briefly feel guilty for it, which is usually a sign that life is beginning to re-enter the room.

The point is not to become untouched by what happened. Some things should leave marks. The point is to survive the first impact without letting it consume the rest of you. Simplify the load, find a mission, stay near people, and remember that the feeling will move.

That is not a grand philosophy. It is just what I have learned from being knocked over a few times and eventually getting back up. Each time has been a little easier. Not because the events mattered less, but because I knew what to do with the first few weeks.

You do not need to solve the whole future while the wound is still open. You just need to make the next day survivable.
